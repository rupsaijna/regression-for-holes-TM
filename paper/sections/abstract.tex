Regression with the Tsetlin Machine (RegressionTM) is interpretable but typically
requires densely sampled training data, raising the question of how well it can be
trained when data is scarce. We study teacher--student knowledge distillation for
RegressionTM, asking not only whether a frozen, better-informed teacher can improve a
data-starved student, but where and why. Rather than matching outputs, our teacher acts
inside the student's training loop: at each sample it adjusts the student's per-sample
error before the Type~I/Type~II feedback decision, steering the clause-level update
directly. We develop a family of such mechanisms---a same-side error blend, adaptive
triggering, opposite-side feedback-type overrides, and teacher-bounded update
magnitudes---and evaluate them under a masked-distillation protocol in which nested
structured holes carve progressively sparser training sets, paired by seed on held-out
data across datasets spanning the full range of teacher quality. We find that every
mechanism scales whatever signal the teacher carries and none rescues a poor teacher;
that teacher accuracy does not predict benefit; that a 70\%-data guided student can match
or even beat a full-data model where extra data is harmful; and that data spacing and
density, not teacher accuracy, govern benefit. Distillation in RegressionTM is thus a
predominantly \emph{global} reshaping of the learned function---helping when a dataset's
error is not spacing-governed and failing when it is---while a sufficiently accurate
teacher additionally repairs the specific regions whose training support was removed.
