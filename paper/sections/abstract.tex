Regression with the Tsetlin Machine (RegressionTM) offers an interpretable, logic-based alternative to conventional numeric predictors, but learning an accurate regression surface typically requires densely sampled training data, raising the question of how well such models can be trained when data is scarce. In this work, we study teacher–student knowledge distillation for RegressionTM, focusing on both whether a frozen, better-informed teacher can improve a data-starved student, and also where and why it does so. We introduce a family of teacher-guided feedback mechanisms that operate inside the student's training loop: at each training sample, the teacher's encoded prediction adjusts the student's per-sample error before the Type I/Type II feedback decision, letting the teacher act on the clause-level update rather than on the output. Building on a simple same-side error blend, we develop and evaluate adaptive triggering, opposite-side feedback-type overrides, and teacher-bounded update magnitudes, benchmarking them under a controlled masked-distillation protocol in which nested structured holes carve progressively sparser training sets. Evaluation is paired by seed on held-out data across datasets that span the full range of teacher quality. 

Our findings across experiments on multiple datasets include: each mechanism acts as a gain on the teacher's per-sample correction rather than repairing the teacher's errors, teacher accuracy does not predict benefit, a 70\%-data guided student can match or exceed a full-data model, and data spacing and density govern benefit.

Distillation in RegressionTM thus behaves as a predominantly global reshaping of the
learned function that a sufficiently accurate teacher supplements with local repair of the
regions whose training support was removed, while the clause-level feedback of the TM provides a transparent mechanism for inspecting and steering what the teacher transfers.
