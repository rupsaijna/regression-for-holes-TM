\subsection{Data spacing and density govern benefit}
\label{sec:spacing}

The recurring threads---teacher accuracy fails to predict benefit, more data is not always
better---point to data spacing/density as the governing variable, which is also the study's
original motivating question (does greater spacing between data points lead to poorer
learning of the regression line?). We test this at two granularities.

\subsubsection{Per-dataset: which datasets benefit}

We run the full mode family over the twelve-dataset panel (large datasets subsampled to
$8000$ training rows) and correlate each mode's fractional benefit against a battery of
density/spacing metrics and against the teacher edge (Spearman across datasets). The
headline result is in Table~\ref{tab:tierA} and visualised in Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}: for
every mode, a density/structure metric out-predicts teacher accuracy, which sits at
$\approx\!0$. The strongest and most robust predictor is \texttt{spc\_err\_strength}---how
strongly local spacing predicts error within a dataset---which correlates with
\texttt{force\_ii\_tmag} benefit at $\rho = -0.78$ ($p=0.003$).

The sign is consistently negative: the more a dataset's error is governed by spacing, the
less distillation helps. On an $n\!\geq\!100$ subset (ten datasets) the
\texttt{force\_ii\_tmag} result tightens (\texttt{spc\_err\_strength} $-0.76$, $p=0.011$)
and a whole cluster of spacing/spread metrics turns negative (spread $-0.62$,
\texttt{nn1\_mean} $-0.57$, \texttt{min\_pairwise} $-0.57$). The robust coupling is
specifically \texttt{force\_ii\_tmag}; the other modes are directionally consistent but
weaker at $n=10$--$12$ (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsubsection{Per-region: where distillation acts}

Bucketing held-out test points by their local spacing (distance to the nearest surviving
training point) tests whether benefit concentrates where data is sparse. Three findings
emerge. First (B1), error rises with spacing, but only at the extreme (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacing},
left): the Spearman correlation of error with spacing is weak (ccpp $+0.02$ to energy
$+0.20$) because error is roughly flat through the bulk and jumps only in the sparsest
decile (sparse penalty: ccpp $+12\%$, airquality $+69\%$, energy $+124\%$). The founding
hypothesis holds at the extreme---the sparsest points are hardest, for the teacher
too---but not through the bulk.

Second (B2), benefit does not concentrate in sparse regions (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacing},
right): the Spearman of benefit against spacing is $\approx\!0$ everywhere. The sign of
benefit is set by the dataset/teacher and is spatially diffuse: ccpp is positive in every
bucket, california and energy negative in every bucket, and airquality actually helps least
in the sparsest bucket---the opposite of gap-filling.

Third (B3), a good teacher nonetheless repairs its own holes. Splitting test points by
whether their specific local support was carved out (nearest removed train point closer
than nearest surviving one; in-hole fraction $\approx\!0.28$--$0.32$) reveals what the
continuous spacing axis missed: on the good teacher ccpp, benefit is
${\sim}1.5$--$2\times$ higher in-hole (\texttt{force\_ii} $+0.397$ in / $+0.194$ out;
\texttt{teacher\_sign\_tmag} $+0.458$ / $+0.312$). This localized repair is absent for the
weak, neutral, and bad teachers (airquality helps less in-hole; california and energy
equal-or-worse in-hole). The structured holes carve $k$-NN neighborhoods out of dense
regions, so in-hole points sit at moderate spacing, not in the sparse tail---the binary
``was your support removed?'' separates them where the continuous spacing axis does not.
