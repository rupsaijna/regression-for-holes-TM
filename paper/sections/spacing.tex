\subsection{Data spacing and density govern benefit}
\label{sec:spacing}

The recurring threads, namely that teacher accuracy fails to predict benefit and that more
data is not always better, point to data spacing and density as the governing variable.
This is also the study's original motivating question: does greater spacing between data
points lead to poorer learning of the regression surface? We test it at two granularities.

\subsubsection{Per-dataset: which datasets benefit}

We run the full mode family over the twelve-dataset panel (large datasets strided to $8000$
training rows) and correlate each mode's fractional benefit with a battery of
density and spacing metrics and with the teacher edge (Spearman across datasets). The
headline result appears in Table~\ref{tab:tierA} and is visualised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}: for every mode, a density or structure metric out-predicts teacher
accuracy, which sits at $\approx\!0$. The strongest and most robust predictor is
\texttt{spc\_err\_strength}, a measure of how strongly local spacing predicts error within
a dataset, which correlates with \texttt{force\_ii\_tmag} benefit at $\rho = -0.78$
($p=0.003$).

The sign is consistently negative: the more a dataset's error is governed by spacing, the
less distillation helps. On an $n\!\geq\!100$ subset (ten datasets) the
\texttt{force\_ii\_tmag} result tightens (\texttt{spc\_err\_strength} $-0.76$, $p=0.011$)
and a cluster of spacing and spread metrics turns negative (\texttt{spread} $-0.62$,
\texttt{nn1\_mean} $-0.57$, \texttt{min\_pairwise} $-0.57$). The robust coupling is specific
to \texttt{force\_ii\_tmag}; the other modes are directionally consistent but weaker at
$n=10$ to $12$ (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsubsection{Per-region: where distillation acts}

We now sort the held-out test points of a dataset by their local spacing (distance to the
nearest surviving training point) and split them into ten equal-count buckets, or deciles,
from densest to sparsest. This lets us ask whether benefit concentrates where data is
sparse. Three findings emerge.

First (B1), error rises with spacing, but only at the extreme (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacing},
left): the Spearman correlation of error with spacing is weak (ccpp $+0.02$ to energy
$+0.20$) because error is roughly flat across the bulk and rises only in the sparsest decile
(sparse penalty: ccpp $+12\%$, airquality $+69\%$, energy $+124\%$). The founding hypothesis
holds at the extreme, where the sparsest points are hardest for the student and for the
teacher alike, but not through the bulk.

Second (B2), benefit does not concentrate in sparse regions (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacing},
right): the Spearman of benefit against spacing is $\approx\!0$ everywhere. The sign of the
benefit is set by the dataset and teacher and is spatially diffuse: ccpp is positive in
every bucket, california and energy negative in every bucket, and airquality helps least in
the sparsest bucket, the opposite of gap-filling.

Third (B3), a good teacher nonetheless repairs its own holes. We split the test points by
whether their specific local support was carved out, that is, whether the nearest removed
training point is closer than the nearest surviving one (in-hole fraction
$\approx\!0.28$ to $0.32$). This reveals what the continuous spacing axis missed: on the
good teacher ccpp, benefit is roughly $1.5$ to $2\times$ higher in-hole (\texttt{force\_ii}
$+0.397$ in versus $+0.194$ out; \texttt{teacher\_sign\_tmag} $+0.458$ versus $+0.312$).
This localized repair is absent for the weak, neutral, and bad teachers (airquality helps
less in-hole; california and energy are equal or worse in-hole). The structured holes carve
$k$-NN neighbourhoods out of dense regions, so in-hole points sit at moderate spacing rather
than in the sparse tail, and the binary test of whether a point's support was removed
separates them where the continuous spacing axis does not.
