\section{Feedback type, stability, and the asymmetry of Type~I/II}
\label{sec:stability}

The opposite-side override experiments expose why the two feedback types behave so
differently under distillation.

\paragraph{\texttt{force\_ii} is safe; \texttt{teacher\_sign} is high-ceiling but
unstable.}
Forcing the bounded Type~II on disagreement samples turns the weak-teacher dataset
airquality into a significant win ($+0.175$, $t=2.33$; gain $+0.098$, $t=2.27$) by
\emph{acting} on samples the baseline ignored, and it never explodes---Type~II can only
erode, so the update is self-limiting. Routing by the teacher's sign achieves the best
single result on ccpp ($+0.529$, $t=3.09$) but \textbf{blows up on airquality} (mean
$-18.3$, individual seeds at $-5, -21, -80$). The mechanism of the blow-up is precisely
the unbounded feedback type: on a disagreement sample where the student over-predicts
(error $>0$) but the teacher under-predicts, \texttt{teacher\_sign} fires \textbf{Type~I},
pushing an already-too-high prediction higher---a positive-feedback loop with no upper
bound. \texttt{force\_ii} is immune because it never selects the raising direction.

\paragraph{Bounding either the type or the magnitude removes the runaway.}
Two fixes both eliminate the instability (airquality standard deviation drops from $25.4$
to $\approx 0.23$). Dropping the Type~I branch (\texttt{teacher\_sign\_ii}) is \emph{safe
but inert}---it kills the blow-up and most of the signal (airquality $\approx 0$, energy
still significantly harmful). Bounding the \emph{magnitude} to the teacher's frozen error
(\texttt{teacher\_sign\_tmag}) is better on every axis: airquality $+0.107$ (actually
positive), energy harm roughly halved ($-0.152$ vs $-0.377$), and the lowest variance
throughout (Table~\ref{tab:stability}). This was the first mechanism in the arc to make a
\emph{bad} teacher meaningfully \emph{less} harmful rather than merely amplifying it.

The broader lesson is that the TM's \textbf{feedback asymmetry is a safety property}: any
distillation mode confined to the bounded Type~II direction is inherently stable, whereas
modes that can trigger the expansive Type~I in the wrong direction require an explicit
magnitude bound.
