\subsection{Feedback type, stability, and the asymmetry of Type~I/II}
\label{sec:stability}

The opposite-side override experiments expose why the two feedback types behave so
differently under distillation (Table~\ref{tab:stability}).

\paragraph{\texttt{force\_ii} is stable; \texttt{teacher\_sign} is high-ceiling but
unstable.}
Forcing the bounded Type~II on disagreement samples turns the weak teacher airquality into
a significant gain ($+0.175$, $t=2.33$) by acting on samples the baseline ignored, and it
never diverges---Type~II can only erode, so the update is self-limiting. Routing by the
teacher's sign (\texttt{teacher\_sign}) achieves the best single result on ccpp ($+0.529$,
$t=3.09$) but diverges on airquality (mean $-17.4$, individual seeds at $-5, -21, -80$;
standard deviation $25.4$). The mechanism of the divergence is precisely the unbounded
feedback type: on a disagreement sample where the student over-predicts (error $>0$) but
the teacher under-predicts, \texttt{teacher\_sign} fires Type~I, pushing an
already-too-high prediction higher---a positive-feedback loop with no upper bound.
\texttt{force\_ii} is immune because it never selects the raising direction.

\paragraph{Bounding either the type or the magnitude removes the divergence.}
Two fixes both eliminate the instability (airquality standard deviation drops from $25.4$
to $\approx\!0.23$; Table~\ref{tab:stability}). Dropping the Type~I branch
(\texttt{teacher\_sign\_ii}) is safe but inert---it removes the divergence and most of the
signal (airquality $\approx\!0$, energy still significantly harmful). Bounding the
magnitude to the teacher's frozen error (\texttt{teacher\_sign\_tmag}) is better on every
axis: airquality $+0.107$ (positive), energy harm roughly halved ($-0.152$ vs $-0.377$),
and the lowest variance throughout. This was the first mechanism we found that makes a bad
teacher meaningfully less harmful rather than merely amplifying it.

The broader lesson is that the TM's feedback asymmetry is a safety property: any
distillation mode confined to the bounded Type~II direction is inherently stable, whereas
modes that can trigger the expansive Type~I in the wrong direction require an explicit
magnitude bound.
