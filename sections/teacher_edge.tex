\section{Teacher accuracy does not predict benefit}
\label{sec:teacher_edge}

A natural gate for distillation would be ``distill only when the teacher is more accurate
than the student.'' We show this gate is wrong. Measuring the teacher's standalone
\emph{edge}---$\mathrm{RMSE}(\text{unguided}) - \mathrm{RMSE}(\text{M20})$ on the same
held-out test---against whether guidance helps (Table~\ref{tab:edge}) reveals that
\textbf{energy, the dataset with the largest teacher edge ($+17\%$, teacher beats the
student in all 6 seeds), is the dataset where every guidance mode hurts most}. ccpp has a
smaller edge ($+6\%$) yet benefits across all modes. Across the four datasets the
edge$\leftrightarrow$benefit Spearman correlation is only $\approx +0.40$ (weakly
positive, $n=4$, not predictive), with energy the off-diagonal point that breaks any
usable threshold.

The reason is that aggregate test RMSE is a \emph{global} summary, whereas our
distillation injects the teacher's \emph{per-sample} error direction and magnitude into
the student's clause feedback. A globally smoother teacher can still deliver per-sample
nudges that conflict with what the masked-region samples need. This pushes the search for
a real predictor of benefit toward the \emph{structure of the data itself}.
